Finding Your Direction

Thinking about starting the college journey? This page will help you familiarize yourself with key terminology you’ll encounter and provide an overview of what’s ahead. Throughout our culture, we’re consistently reminded of our people’s resilience. How our ancestors overcame many incredible obstacles, persevered, and, through their sacrifices, we now stand here with the freedom to choose what we want our lives to be.
By choosing to pursue education, you’ve chosen a path to align yourself with the stars, the earth, and the sky. To regain balance and explore a new level of understanding about what it means to be an indigenous person in today’s world. If you’re still unsure, that’s ok. It’s a big step, and if this is your first time researching it, it might feel overwhelming at first. Just remember that you are an extension of those who came before you. When you need it most, just channel that strength to help guide you.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is FAFSA?

Every year, a form is released called FAFSA. It asks for your family’s financial information. Think of it as taking a snapshot of your family’s current spending and income habits. This form is then sent to a group of people who determine how much college they think you can afford based on those details.

This affordability number is then sent to your school. This number determines a variety of factors, such as scholarships, grants, and work-study programs for which you may be eligible.

How do I afford college?

That’s a big question, and it can be extremely intimidating, but don’t worry—you have options.

Tribal Colleges and Partnerships

Many colleges offer free tuition options for members of a federally recognized tribe. There are community colleges and universities that specialize in tribal connections and ensuring your experiences allow you the best of both worlds. Tuition can either be discounted or fully covered.

Scholarships

One option is scholarships. There are scholarship portals where you provide details about yourself, and then you are matched with possible scholarships for which you are eligible. Scholarships are awarded based on a variety of factors: merit (like your GPA or test scores), charity/volunteer experience, leadership projects, and your major.

These applications typically involve filling out forms with your personal details, essays, photos, and proof of a required document (if applicable).

How do I start?

Choose a school. Where do you want to go? In-state students typically pay lower tuition compared to out-of-state students. Don’t forget to check out tribal colleges and universities, as they often provide help with tuition costs. Don’t get overwhelmed, you’ll figure the rest out later. Starting here will keep you focused on the goal. There are a lot of questions you need to have answered, and a lot of them will be based on the school you’re going to choose. Once the school has been decided, you can proceed to the next step and visit the Walking the Path page for additional assistance.

Types of Higher Education Pathways

Degree Pathway What You Need To Know Length of Time
Certificate Focusing on skill or training, it’s great to get into a skilled trade more quickly. 6 months to 12 months
Associate’s Degree Usually earned at a community college. Starting here can be an affordable option if you plan to continue earning your bachelor's degree, as your associate’s degree can typically be transferred into a bachelor's program. 2 years
Bachelor’s Degree Usually earned at a university. Often required for specialized positions that require more time and study. An associate’s degree can help complete the first two years of this program. 4 years
Master’s Degree You must have a bachelor’s degree to begin. A master’s degree helps you build expertise through advanced study — essentially becoming a “master” in your field. 1 to 2 years
Doctoral Degree This is the highest degree you can earn. It makes you a professional in the field and often requires extensive research and practical experience. Varies
